# 01-foundational-grandmasters

Purpose: collect the historical and theoretical anchors behind the shipbuilding grandmasters framework.

## William Froude: similitude, model testing, resistance decomposition

Source: Britannica, "William Froude"
URL: https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Froude
Reliability: secondary but edited reference.

Key extraction:
- Froude influenced ship design by developing model tests in water and extrapolating model results to full-scale ships when geometrical similarity holds.
- He proposed model experiments to the British Admiralty, and a test tank was built at Torquay around 1870.
- He separated the chief resistance components into skin friction and wave formation.

Distilled principle:
- Never judge a hull only by appearance. Ask which nondimensional similarity is being preserved and which resistance component is being scaled or corrected.
- Model tests are not "small ships"; they are controlled similarity arguments.

Use in final skill:
- Add a Froude gate: check Froude number, Reynolds number, scale effects, resistance decomposition, and model-ship correlation.

## David W. Taylor: systematic model-basin empiricism

Source: NAVSEA / NSWC Carderock biography
URL: https://www.navsea.navy.mil/Home/Warfare-Centers/NSWC-Carderock/Who-We-Are/Rear-Adm-David-W-Taylor/
Reliability: official institutional source.

Key extraction:
- Taylor designed and supervised the Washington Navy Yard Experimental Model Basin.
- He ran experiments to determine which hull characteristics govern water resistance.
- The Taylor Standard Series Method enabled advance estimation of resistance for ships of given proportions.
- More than 1,000 ship designs were tested during his period at the Experimental Model Basin.

Distilled principle:
- Use systematic variation, not isolated examples.
- Good empirical design is not anecdote; it is a controlled family of hulls with known parameter ranges.

Use in final skill:
- Add a design-lane check: if using a series method or regression, identify the parameter range and whether the target hull belongs to the family.

## J. H. Michell and thin-ship theory: wave resistance as a calculable mechanism

Representative source: later literature identifying Michell's 1898 thin-ship theory as the basis of wave-resistance calculation.
Search result anchor: "Michell's Thin Ship Theory Considering the Gradient of Ship Form in Depth Direction"
URL: https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/jjasnaoe/19/0/19_19/_article
Reliability: peer-reviewed derivative source; original 1898 paper should be preferred when available.

Key extraction:
- Thin-ship theory treats wave-making resistance through a linearized theoretical model.
- It is useful as a mechanism-level explanation, not a universal full-form prediction tool.

Distilled principle:
- Separate "what physics creates the force" from "how accurately this particular approximation predicts it."

Use in final skill:
- Add a physics-check step: ask what part of resistance is viscous, wave-making, appendage, air, added resistance, or interaction.

## J. N. Newman: unified marine hydrodynamics

Source: MIT Press, "Marine Hydrodynamics"
URL: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262534826/marine-hydrodynamics/
Reliability: publisher page for a canonical textbook.

Key extraction:
- Newman's book takes a balanced approach between theory and empirics, giving theoretical background for intelligent evaluation and application of empirical procedures.
- It unifies marine problems as related applications of hydrodynamics.
- Contents include model testing, viscous and ideal fluid motion, lifting surfaces, waves, wave effects, and slender bodies.

Distilled principle:
- Empirical methods are not replacements for physics. They need theoretical understanding to know when they apply.

Use in final skill:
- Add a "theory + empirics" rule: require both a physical mechanism and an evidence source for engineering claims.
